Cygnus X-1 · Nature Astronomy · 2026

Black hole jets, measured in motion.

For the first time, astronomers used a black hole jet being bent by a companion star’s wind to estimate its instantaneous power and speed, instead of averaging over tens of thousands of years. The target was Cygnus X-1, one of the most famous black hole systems in the sky.

Why this matters

A live-style read on black hole power

Usually, black hole jet power is inferred from giant bubbles and shocks blown into surrounding gas, which means averaging over immense timescales. This study used the jet’s actual bend, caused by the donor star’s wind, to estimate what the jet is doing now.

The jet is not just blasting into empty space. It has to punch through the powerful stellar wind of the black hole’s companion, and that sideways shove leaves a measurable geometric fingerprint.

The big idea

How a bent jet becomes a measuring tool

If you know how hard the stellar wind is pushing, and you can see how much the jet bends, you can work backwards to estimate the jet’s own momentum and energy flow.

1. The donor star throws off a fierce wind

Cygnus X-1’s companion is a massive O-type supergiant. The black hole is accreting from that wind, but the same wind also acts like a crosswind on the outgoing jet.

2. The jet bends away from the star

High-resolution radio images showed the approaching and receding jets are not perfectly collinear. Their orbital-phase-dependent position angle shifts line up with a wind-bending picture.

3. Geometry reveals power

A stronger, faster jet resists bending. A weaker one gets shoved more. That tradeoff lets the bending angle act like a proxy for instantaneous jet momentum flux and power.

Scale

What makes this system special

Cygnus X-1 is a rare sweet spot: powerful enough to launch a stable compact jet, nearby enough for VLBI radio imaging, and embedded in a massive stellar wind that actually deforms the outflow.

Fast compared with human intuition

These jets are relativistic outflows. In the paper, speed is constrained from the radio jet properties and the brightness asymmetry of approaching versus receding sides, in combination with the bending model.

Short compared with galactic monsters

This is not a giant extragalactic radio lobe tens of thousands of light years wide. It is a much smaller black hole binary, which is exactly why the jet’s changing geometry can be tied to present-day conditions.

Important beyond one object

Cygnus X-1 has been used to anchor scaling relations between radio brightness and jet power across black holes of wildly different masses. An instantaneous measurement helps sharpen that whole picture.

What the paper did

From radio images to physical inference

The study reanalysed a 2016 VLBI campaign spanning nearly a full 5.6-day orbit, detecting both the approaching and receding jets in each observation and verifying the bending signal against archival data.

Observe over orbital phase

VLBA and EVN observations tracked the jet while the black hole moved around the donor star, letting the apparent jet angle be compared across orbital phases.

Show the jets bend away from the star

The approaching and receding sides do not line up like a simple straight precessing jet. That asymmetry is exactly what a wind-bending scenario predicts.

Model the jet-wind momentum balance

The authors balance stellar wind momentum against jet lateral momentum flux, with orbital motion included, to infer instantaneous jet properties.

Why this beats old-style averages

  • Bubble calorimetry smooths over enormous timescales.
  • Accretion flows vary much faster than the inflated cavities they leave behind.
  • A bent compact jet provides a geometry tied to the current state of the system.
  • That makes the result much more useful for feedback efficiency calibration.

In plain English: instead of measuring the fossil record of black hole activity, this method reads something much closer to the live stream.
